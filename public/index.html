Stock Market 101

What are stocks, how exchanges work, and why prices move

What Is a Stock?

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents a fractional ownership stake in a corporation. When you buy a share of a company listed on the Jamaica Stock Exchange, you become a part-owner of that business and are entitled to a proportional claim on its assets and earnings.

Companies issue stock to raise capital for expansion, research, paying down debt, or other corporate purposes. Investors buy stocks in the hope that the company will grow in value over time, allowing them to sell their shares at a higher price than they paid, or to receive a portion of the company's profits through dividends.

How Does a Stock Exchange Work?

A stock exchange is a regulated marketplace where buyers and sellers come together to trade shares of publicly listed companies. The Jamaica Stock Exchange (JSE), founded in 1968, serves this function for Jamaica.

When you place a buy order through your broker, it is matched with a corresponding sell order. The exchange ensures that trades are executed fairly and transparently, with prices determined by supply and demand. If more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price rises. If more people want to sell, the price falls.

Key concept: Stock prices are ultimately driven by supply and demand, which in turn are influenced by company earnings, economic conditions, interest rates, investor sentiment, and market liquidity.
Why Do Stock Prices Move?
  • Company earnings: If a company reports higher-than-expected profits, its stock price typically rises as investors become more optimistic about its future.
  • Economic data: GDP growth, inflation reports, employment figures, and central bank interest rate decisions all affect investor confidence and stock valuations.
  • Industry trends: Changes in an industry — such as new regulations, technological disruption, or shifts in consumer demand — can move stock prices for all companies in that sector.
  • Investor sentiment: Fear, greed, and herd behaviour can cause prices to overshoot or undershoot a stock's fundamental value in the short term.
  • Liquidity: On smaller exchanges like the JSE, some stocks trade infrequently, meaning a single large buy or sell order can move the price significantly.

Reading Financial Statements

Income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow explained

The Three Core Financial Statements

Every publicly listed company is required to publish financial statements, typically on a quarterly and annual basis. Understanding these three statements is essential for evaluating whether a stock is a good investment.

1. Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

The income statement shows a company's revenues, expenses, and profit over a specific period. It answers the question: "Is this company making money?"

  • Revenue (Sales): The total amount of money the company earned from selling its products or services.
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The direct costs of producing the goods or services sold.
  • Gross Profit: Revenue minus COGS. This shows how efficiently the company produces its goods.
  • Operating Expenses: Costs of running the business (salaries, rent, marketing, etc.).
  • Net Income: The bottom line — profit after all expenses, interest, and taxes have been deducted.
2. Balance Sheet

The balance sheet provides a snapshot of what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual value belonging to shareholders (equity) at a specific point in time.

The fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
  • Assets: Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, property, equipment, and intangible assets like patents.
  • Liabilities: Debts, accounts payable, bonds issued, and other obligations.
  • Shareholders' Equity: The net worth of the company — what would be left if all assets were sold and all debts paid.
3. Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of the business. A company can report a profit on its income statement while still running out of cash, which is why this statement is critical.

  • Operating Cash Flow: Cash generated from the company's core business operations.
  • Investing Cash Flow: Cash spent on or received from buying/selling long-term assets (equipment, acquisitions).
  • Financing Cash Flow: Cash from issuing stock or debt, and cash paid out as dividends or for share buybacks.

Technical Analysis Basics

Support/resistance, moving averages, and RSI explained

What Is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is the study of historical price and volume data to forecast future price movements. Unlike fundamental analysis (which focuses on a company's financial health), technical analysis is based on the idea that price patterns tend to repeat because market psychology is consistent over time.

Support and Resistance

Support is a price level where a stock tends to stop falling because buying interest is strong enough to absorb selling pressure. Think of it as a floor. Resistance is a price level where a stock tends to stop rising because selling pressure overcomes buying interest. Think of it as a ceiling.

When a stock breaks through a resistance level, that level often becomes the new support, and vice versa. These levels are identified by looking at previous price highs and lows on a chart.

Moving Averages

A moving average smooths out price data by calculating the average price over a specified number of periods. The two most common types are:

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): The arithmetic mean of the closing prices over a set number of periods (e.g., 50-day SMA, 200-day SMA).
  • Exponential Moving Average (EMA): Gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information.
Golden Cross: When a shorter-term moving average (e.g., 50-day) crosses above a longer-term one (e.g., 200-day), it is considered a bullish signal.
Death Cross: The opposite — when the short-term average crosses below the long-term one, it is considered bearish.
Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a scale from 0 to 100. It was developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. in 1978.

  • RSI above 70: The stock may be overbought — its price may have risen too fast and could be due for a pullback.
  • RSI below 30: The stock may be oversold — its price may have fallen too fast and could be due for a bounce.

RSI is most useful when combined with other indicators and not relied upon in isolation.

Understanding Dividends

Yield, ex-dates, and dividend reinvestment

What Are Dividends?

A dividend is a distribution of a portion of a company's earnings to its shareholders. Not all companies pay dividends — many high-growth companies prefer to reinvest all profits back into the business. However, many established Jamaican companies (particularly in banking, insurance, and conglomerates) pay regular dividends.

Dividend Yield

The dividend yield is calculated as the annual dividend per share divided by the current share price, expressed as a percentage. For example, if a stock pays J$5.00 per share annually and trades at J$100.00, the dividend yield is 5%.

Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend per Share / Current Share Price) × 100

A high yield can be attractive, but it may also indicate that the stock price has fallen sharply, which could signal underlying problems with the company.

Key Dividend Dates
  • Declaration Date: The date the company's board of directors announces the dividend.
  • Ex-Dividend Date: The cutoff date — you must own the stock before this date to receive the dividend. On the ex-date, the stock price typically drops by approximately the dividend amount.
  • Record Date: The date the company checks its records to determine who the shareholders of record are.
  • Payment Date: The date the dividend is actually paid to shareholders.
Dividend Reinvestment

Dividend reinvestment means using the dividends you receive to buy additional shares of the same stock. Over time, this compounds your returns because you earn dividends on an ever-increasing number of shares. Many investors consider this one of the most powerful wealth-building strategies for long-term investors.

Risk Management

Diversification, position sizing, and stop losses

Why Risk Management Matters

Investing without risk management is gambling. Even the best stock picks can lose value due to unforeseen events. Risk management is the practice of identifying, assessing, and controlling the financial risks in your portfolio to protect your capital and achieve more consistent returns.

Diversification

Diversification means spreading your investments across different stocks, sectors, asset classes, and even geographies so that poor performance in one area does not devastate your entire portfolio.

  • Sector diversification: Don't put all your money into one industry. If you hold only banking stocks and the financial sector declines, your entire portfolio suffers.
  • Asset class diversification: Combine stocks with bonds, real estate, and cash equivalents. Different asset classes often behave differently during economic cycles.
  • Geographic diversification: Investing in both Jamaican and international markets reduces your exposure to any single country's economic risks.
Position Sizing

Position sizing is determining how much of your total portfolio to allocate to any single investment. A common guideline is to limit any single stock position to no more than 5-10% of your total portfolio. This way, even if one stock drops significantly, the impact on your overall portfolio is contained.

Stop Losses

A stop-loss order is an instruction to your broker to sell a stock if it falls to a specified price. For example, if you buy a stock at J$100 and set a stop loss at J$85, the stock will be automatically sold if it drops to J$85, limiting your loss to 15%.

Important: On less liquid markets like the JSE, stop-loss orders may not always execute at the exact price specified, especially for thinly traded stocks. Always consider a stock's daily trading volume before setting stops.

The Jamaica Stock Exchange

History, market hours, how to start investing, and major sectors

History of the JSE

The Jamaica Stock Exchange was established on 3 February 1969. It is one of the oldest stock exchanges in the English-speaking Caribbean. The exchange has grown from a handful of listed companies to over 100 securities across its Main Market, Junior Market, and USD Equities Market.

The JSE has been recognized internationally for its strong performance. In multiple years (notably 2015, 2018, and 2019), it was ranked among the top-performing stock exchanges in the world by Bloomberg based on local currency returns.

Market Hours

The JSE operates Monday through Friday (excluding public holidays). Regular trading hours are 9:30 AM to 1:30 PM Jamaica Time (EST). Pre-market indicative pricing opens at 9:00 AM.

How to Open a Brokerage Account

To buy and sell shares on the JSE, you need an account with a licensed broker-dealer. The major brokers in Jamaica include:

  • JMMB Securities Limited — One of the largest financial groups in the Caribbean, offering online trading.
  • Barita Investments Limited — A full-service wealth management and brokerage firm.
  • Mayberry Investments Limited — Known for its Mayberry Online Trading (MOT) platform.
  • NCB Capital Markets — The brokerage arm of National Commercial Bank Jamaica.
  • Scotia Investments Jamaica Limited — Part of the Scotiabank Group.
  • VM Wealth Management — Affiliated with the Victoria Mutual Group.

To open an account, you typically need a valid government-issued ID (passport or national ID), proof of address (utility bill), a TRN (Taxpayer Registration Number), and an initial deposit (minimum amounts vary by broker).

Major Sectors on the JSE
  • Financial Services: Banks, insurance companies, and investment firms (e.g., NCB Financial Group, Sagicor Group, GraceKennedy Financial).
  • Manufacturing & Distribution: Food, beverages, and consumer goods (e.g., Seprod, Wisynco Group, Caribbean Cement).
  • Conglomerates: Diversified holdings companies (e.g., GraceKennedy, Jamaica Producers Group).
  • Tourism & Hospitality: Hotels and tourism-related businesses.
  • Technology: A growing sector, particularly on the Junior Market, including IT services and fintech companies.

JSE Market Hours

The Jamaica Stock Exchange operates Monday through Friday, excluding Jamaican public holidays.

JSE Market Structure

The Jamaica Stock Exchange operates three distinct markets:

MarketDescriptionRequirements
Main MarketFor established, larger companies with a longer operating historyMinimum shareholders' equity of J$500 million; at least 3 years of audited financials
Junior MarketDesigned for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) seeking to raise capital and growMinimum shareholders' equity of J$50 million; tax incentive — companies pay 0% corporate income tax for the first 5 years after listing
USD Equities MarketAllows companies to list and trade shares denominated in US dollarsProvides access to USD-denominated investment opportunities for local and international investors

Trading Fees and Taxes

When you buy or sell stocks on the JSE, the following fees and taxes typically apply:

  • Brokerage Commission: Typically 1% to 2% of the transaction value (varies by broker; some offer lower rates for larger trades or online orders).
  • JCSD Fee: The Jamaica Central Securities Depository charges a small fee for the electronic settlement of trades (usually about 0.0945% of the transaction value).
  • GCT (General Consumption Tax): 15% GCT is charged on the brokerage commission (not on the trade value itself).
  • Transfer Tax: A stamp duty / transfer tax of approximately J$100 per transaction may apply.
  • Withholding Tax on Dividends: Dividend income received from Jamaican stocks is subject to a 15% withholding tax, which is deducted at source by the company paying the dividend.
  • Capital Gains Tax: As of the current tax regime, Jamaica does not impose a capital gains tax on profits from the sale of listed securities. However, tax laws are subject to change — always verify with a tax professional or the Tax Administration Jamaica (TAJ).
